Source: Core English Grammar Reference (A1-B2 Level) / British Council Grammar Reference

## 1. "TO BE" (Am / Is / Are)

| Subject | Full form | Contraction | Negative | Neg. contraction |
|---------|-----------|-------------|----------|-----------------|
| I | I am | I**'m** | I am not | I**'m not** (⚠ NOT "I amn't") |
| He/She/It | He is | He**'s** | He is not | He**'s not** / He **isn't** |
| You/We/They | You are | You**'re** | You are not | You**'re not** / You **aren't** |

**Questions:** invert subject and verb → *Am I? / Is she? / Are they?*

**Short answers:**
| Question | Yes | No |
|----------|-----|----|
| Am I late? | Yes, you are. | No, you're not. |
| Is he tired? | Yes, he is. | No, he isn't. |
| Are they here? | Yes, they are. | No, they aren't. |

**Question words:** put the question word before the verb:
- *Where **are** you from?* / *When **is** the meeting?* / *What time **is** it?*

**Critical rule:** Turkish uses "sahip olmak" for age, but English always uses "to be":
- ✓ *I am 30 years old.*
- ✗ *I have 30 years.*

## 2. PRESENT SIMPLE (Geniş Zaman)

Used for routines, habits, and general truths.

**Full conjugation:**

| | Affirmative | Negative | Question |
|-|-------------|----------|---------|
| I/You/We/They | I like. | I don't like. | Do I like? |
| He/She/It | He **likes**. | He **doesn't** like. | **Does** he like? |

**Short answers:** *Do you like cheese? — Yes, I do. / No, I don't.* | *Does she play? — Yes, she does. / No, she doesn't.*

**Irregular he/she/it forms:**
- `have → has` (*He **has** a car.*)
- `do → does` (*She **does** yoga.*)
- `go → goes` (*My dog **goes** for a walk.*)

**Question words with do/does:**
- *Where **do** you work?* / *What time **does** he have lunch?*

**3rd person singular `-s` rules:**

| Ending | Rule | Example |
|--------|------|---------|
| `ch, sh, x, ss, o` | add **-es** | watch→watches, go→goes |
| consonant + `y` | drop y, add **-ies** | study→studies |
| vowel + `y` | add **-s** only | play→plays |
| other | add **-s** | live→lives |

## 3. PRESENT CONTINUOUS (Şimdiki Zaman)

Used for actions happening now or temporary situations.

**Structure:** am / is / are + verb**(-ing)**

**Stative Verbs — CANNOT take `-ing`:**  
Verbs expressing feelings, thoughts, or possession use Present Simple instead.

| ✗ Wrong | ✓ Correct |
|---------|-----------|
| I am wanting a coffee. | I **want** a coffee. |
| She is knowing the answer. | She **knows** the answer. |

Common stative verbs: `want, love, hate, know, like, believe, need, have` (possession) → full list: §49

### Advanced uses of present continuous

**Complaints and annoying habits:** `always/constantly/forever + -ing` (emphasises annoyance):
- *My daughter is **always leaving** clothes all over the floor.*
- *He**'s always coming** in late and making noise.*

**Describing change and development:**
- *Life **is becoming** more expensive.*
- *The world **is changing** rapidly.*

### Advanced uses of present simple

**Headlines** (simplified past → present simple in newspaper titles):
- *Billionaire **buys** multinational company*

**Instructions:**
- ***Open** the link and **enter** your password.*

**Plot summaries** (books, films, plays — use present tenses):
- *She **quits** her job and **goes** in search of adventure.*
- *While she **is travelling** in India, she **meets** someone special.*

**Anecdotes** (real stories told vividly — present makes it immediate):
- *So I**'m on** the bus and this guy **comes** up to me and **starts** talking …*

## 4. PAST SIMPLE (Geçmiş Zaman)

Used for completed actions in the past.

**Regular verbs:** add `-ed` → walk→walked, watch→watched  
**Irregular verbs:** form changes entirely → go→went, see→saw, buy→bought

**Question/Negative:** `Did / Didn't` + subject + base verb (verb returns to base form):
- *I didn't go to school.* / *Did you see him?*

**To Be in Past Simple:**

| Present | Past |
|---------|------|
| am / is | **was** |
| are | **were** |

|--------|------|---------|

| consonant + `y` | drop y, add **-ies** | baby→babies |
| `f` or `fe` | drop f/fe, add **-ves** | knife→knives, wolf→wolves |
| other | add **-s** | book→books |

**Irregular plurals:**

| Singular | Plural |
|----------|--------|
| man | men |
| woman | women |
| child | children |
| person | people |
| tooth | teeth |
| foot | feet |
| mouse | mice |

## 6. PRONOUNS & POSSESSIVES

| | Subject | Object | Possessive Adj. | Possessive Pronoun |
|--|---------|--------|-----------------|-------------------|
| 1st sg | I | me | my | mine |
| 2nd | you | you | your | yours |
| 3rd sg m | he | him | his | his |
| 3rd sg f | she | her | her | hers |
| 3rd sg n | it | it | its | — |
| 1st pl | we | us | our | ours |
| 3rd pl | they | them | their | theirs |

**Apostrophe `-'s` for possession:**
- Singular: *Tom**'s** car*
- Plural ending in `s`: *the students**'** books* (apostrophe only, no extra s)
- Irregular plural (doesn't end in s): *the children**'s** books, the women**'s** clothes*
- Name ending in `s`: *James**'** bag* or *James**'s** bag* (both acceptable)
- Multiple possessives in a phrase: *my friend**'s** father**'s** house*
- List of names — `'s` goes on the **last name only**: *Anne and Gary**'s** son*
- ⚠ `s` without apostrophe = **plural**, NOT possession: *I've got two brothers.* (not brothers')

## 8. PREPOSITIONS OF PLACE & TIME

**Place:**

**in** — inside an enclosed or larger space:
- Countries, cities: *in Turkey, in London*
- Rooms, buildings: *in the kitchen, in the box*
- Natural spaces: *in the mountains, in the countryside, in a valley, in the forest*
- Water: *in the sea, in a river, in a pool*
- Vehicles (enclosed): *in a car, in a taxi*

**on** — on a surface or line:
- Surfaces: *on the table, on the wall, on the fifth floor*
- Public transport (not enclosed): *on the bus, on the train, on a plane*
- Lines, rivers, streets, islands: *London is on the River Thames; there's a market on James Street; I'd love to live on the Isle of Wight*

**at** — a specific point or place for an activity:
- Activity locations: *at work, at school, at home, at the party, at the supermarket*
- Addresses and exact positions: *I live at 15 Craig Street; she's sitting at a desk; he's waiting at the entrance*

**Other:**
| Preposition | Use | Example |
|-------------|-----|---------|
| **under** | below | *under the bed* |
| **next to** | beside | *next to the door* |
| **between** | in the middle of two | *between the two chairs* |

**Time — In / On / At:**

**at** — clock times, mealtimes, specific phrases:
- Clock: *at 6.30, at 9 o'clock, at 13.00*
- Meals/breaks: *at breakfast time, at lunchtime, at dinner time*
- Fixed phrases: *at night, at the weekend, at Christmas, at Easter, at Chinese New Year*
- **at night vs in the night:** `at night` = all night (general); `in the night` = a specific moment during the night
  - *She works **at night**.* / *The baby woke up **in the night**.*

**in** — parts of day, months, seasons, years, longer periods:
- Parts of day: *in the morning / afternoon / evening*
- Months: *in January, in May*
- Seasons: *in (the) spring / summer / autumn / winter*
- Years, centuries, decades: *in 2026, in the 21st century, in the 80s*
- Other: *in the past, in the future, in the last few years*

**on** — days and dates:
- Days: *on Monday, on Fridays, on weekdays*
- Dates: *on 5th June, on 30 July*
- Special days: *on my birthday, on New Year's Day*

## 9. ADJECTIVES + PREPOSITIONS

There are no strict grammatical rules for which preposition follows which adjective — learn them as fixed phrases. A preposition is always followed by a noun or gerund (-ing form).

### With **at** — skills and abilities
`good / bad / amazing / brilliant / terrible / experienced at`
- *He's really good at English.*
- *She's amazing at the piano.*
- *I'm not very good at drawing.*

### With **about** — feelings (cause of emotion)
`angry / excited / happy / nervous / sad / stressed / worried about`
- *I'm angry about the decision.*
- *She's excited about the new job.*
- *They were worried about the exam.*

### With **of** — certain fear/pride feelings
`afraid / frightened / scared / proud of`
- *She was afraid of telling her mum.*
- *He's scared of flying.*
- *You should be proud of your progress.*

### With **to** — connection between people or things
`married / addicted / allergic / similar to`
- *He's married to the director.*
- *It's similar to the old one.*

With **to** — behaviour towards others:
`friendly / nice / polite / rude / kind to`
- *They were really friendly to me.*
- *She was very rude to the waitress.*

### With **for** — benefit, reputation, responsibility
`good / bad for` | `famous / responsible for`
- *Exercise is good for you.*
- *The town is famous for its cheese.*
- *I'm responsible for the financial side.*

### With **in** — interest, involvement, experience
`interested / experienced / involved in`
- *She's interested in the project.*
- *They want someone experienced in design.*
- *I didn't want to get involved in the argument.*

## 10. ADJECTIVES ENDING IN -ED AND -ING

These are commonly confused. The rule is consistent:

| Form | Describes | Example |
|------|-----------|---------|
| **-ed** | How a **person feels** (the emotion itself) | *I was bored.* |
| **-ing** | What **causes** the emotion (the thing/situation) | *The lesson was boring.* |

**Key pairs:**

| Person feels (-ed) | Thing causes it (-ing) |
|--------------------|----------------------|
| annoyed | annoying |
| bored | boring |
| confused | confusing |
| disappointed | disappointing |
| excited | exciting |
| frightened | frightening |
| interested | interesting |
| surprised | surprising |
| tired | tiring |
| worried | worrying |

**Examples:**
- *I was so bored in that lesson.* (how I felt)
- *That lesson was really boring.* (what caused the feeling)
- *He was surprised to see Helen.* (his emotion)
- *Have you seen that film? It's frightening.* (the film causes fear)

## 11. ARTICLES: A / AN / THE

### Jobs — always use a/an
- *He's an architect. / She's a scientist. / My grandmother was a teacher.*

### Singular countable nouns — always need an article or determiner

**A/An (indefinite)** — first mention, or one of a type:
- *I saw a good film yesterday.*
- *She has a university degree.* (starts with "y" sound → a)
- *It took me an hour.* ("h" silent → vowel sound → an)

**The (definite)** — listener already knows which one (mentioned before, or unique):
- *I'm going to take the dog for a walk.* (both know which dog)
- *Have you seen the car key?*
- *They go to the school next to the bridge.*

### Things in general — NO article (plural or uncountable)
- *Birds eat worms. / Water freezes at 0°C. / Children need sleep.*

### Particular group of things — THE
- *We went to the zoo and saw the kangaroos.* (those specific kangaroos, not kangaroos in general)

## 12. ARTICLES: THE OR NO ARTICLE

### Common phrases — NO article

**Bed / Work / Home:**
- *go to bed / be in bed*
- *go to work / be at work / start work / finish work*
- *go home / be at home / get home / stay at home*

**School / University / Prison / Hospital** (as participant):
- *start school / go to school / be at school*
- *go to university / be at university*
- *go to prison / be in prison*
- *go to hospital / be in hospital*

**But use THE when visiting as an outsider** (not student/prisoner/patient):
- *I went to **the** school to meet his teacher.*
- *I'm at **the** hospital — my sister just had a baby.*

### Place names — NO article

Continents, most countries, cities, lakes, single mountains, most universities:
- *Africa, Asia, Europe*
- *India, Ghana, Denmark, Peru*
- *New York, Hanoi, Moscow*
- *Lake Victoria, Lake Superior*
- *Mount Everest, Mount Kilimanjaro*
- *Cardiff University, Harvard University*

### Place names — USE THE

| Category | Rule | Examples |
|----------|------|---------|
| Countries with "United" | **the** | *the United Kingdom, the United States, the United Arab Emirates* |
| Other exceptions | **the** | *the Bahamas, the Gambia* |
| Seas, oceans | **the** | *the Atlantic, the Pacific, the Mediterranean* |
| Mountain ranges | **the** | *the Andes, the Himalayas, the Alps* |
| Rivers | **the** | *the Nile, the Amazon, the Yangtze* |
| Universities with "of" | **the** | *the University of Tokyo, the University of Delhi* |

## 13. COMPARATIVE ADJECTIVES

Used to compare two things or show change.

### One-syllable adjectives → add **-er**

| Pattern | Rule | Example |
|---------|------|---------|
| Regular | add **-er** | old→older, clean→cleaner |
| Ends in `-e` | add **-r** | safe→safer, nice→nicer |
| Vowel + consonant | double consonant + **-er** | big→bigger, hot→hotter |

### Two-syllable adjectives

| Pattern | Rule | Example |
|---------|------|---------|
| Ends in consonant + `-y` | change y→i, add **-er** | noisy→noisier, happy→happier |
| Other two-syllable | **more** + adjective | crowded→more crowded |
| Exceptions (either form OK) | `-er` or **more** | simple→simpler / more simple |

*Exceptions (can use either):* common, cruel, gentle, handsome, likely, narrow, pleasant, polite, simple, stupid

### Three or more syllables → always **more**
- *dangerous → more dangerous*
- *interesting → more interesting*

### Irregular comparatives

| Adjective | Comparative |
|-----------|------------|
| good | **better** |
| bad | **worse** |
| far | **further / farther** |

### Than
When specifying what is being compared, use **than**:
- *Their house is cleaner than ours.*
- *Traffic is slower in the city than in the countryside.*
- *After the race I was more tired than Anne.*

## 14. INFINITIVE OF PURPOSE

To answer "why?", use **to + infinitive** (not *for + verb*).

| ✗ Wrong | ✓ Correct |
|---------|-----------|
| I use it *for go* to work. | I use it **to go** to work. |
| She runs *for keep* fit. | She runs **to keep** fit. |

- *He went to the shops **to buy** some shoes.*
- *I'm writing **to ask** for your help.*
- *My father runs every day **to keep** healthy.*
- *Why do you have a bicycle? — **To go** to work.*

**In order to** = more formal alternative; meaning identical:
- *She studied hard **in order to** pass the exam.*

## 15. COUNTABLE AND UNCOUNTABLE NOUNS

### Countable nouns
Can be singular or plural; can use a/an.
- *a cup, two cups; a chair, some chairs*

### Uncountable nouns
No plural form; cannot use a/an; use **some** or no article.
- *water, money, bread, information, advice, furniture, luggage*
- ✗ *a money / two moneys* — ✓ *some money*

### Sentence patterns

**Positive:**
| Noun type | Singular | Plural |
|-----------|----------|--------|
| Countable | *There's **a** man at the door.* | *I have **some** friends.* |
| Uncountable | — | *There's **some** milk in the fridge.* |

**Negative:**
| Noun type | Singular | Plural |
|-----------|----------|--------|
| Countable | *I don't have **a** dog.* | *There aren't **any** seats.* |
| Uncountable | — | *There isn't **any** coffee.* |

**Questions:**
| Noun type | Forms |
|-----------|-------|
| Countable | *Is there **an** email? / Are there **any** chairs? / **How many** chairs?* |
| Uncountable | *Is there **any** sugar? / **How much** orange juice?* |

**Some in offers, requests, and when expecting "yes":**
- *Do you want **some** chocolate?* (offering)
- *Can we have **some** more chairs, please?* (requesting)
- *Have you got **some** new glasses?* (expecting yes)

### Much / Many / A lot of

| Quantifier | Use | Notes |
|------------|-----|-------|
| **a lot of / lots of** | both, positive sentences | *They have **a lot of** money.* |
| **not many** | countable, negatives | *There aren't **many** potatoes.* |
| **not much** | uncountable, negatives | *There isn't **much** water.* |
| **many / much** | questions and formal positives | *How **many** people? / How **much** time?* |

**⚠ Avoid many/much in positive statements — use a lot of instead:**
- ✗ *I have many friends.* (sounds formal/unnatural)
- ✓ *I have **a lot of** friends.*

## 16. PAST CONTINUOUS

Used for actions **in progress** at a point in the past; often provides background to a past simple event.

**Structure:** was / were + verb**(-ing)**

| Subject | Form |
|---------|------|
| I / He / She / It | **was** + -ing |
| You / We / They | **were** + -ing |

**Past simple review:**
- Negative: `didn't + base verb` → *I **didn't wake up** early.*
- Question: `Did + subject + base verb` → *Did you **see** him?*

**Past continuous + past simple (interrupted/background action):**

When these two tenses are used together, the past simple action happened **in the middle of** the past continuous action.

- *When I woke up, it **was snowing**.* (ongoing background)
- *I **was sleeping** when you called me.* (interrupted)
- *While she **was cooking**, the phone rang.*
- *As I **was going** to work, I saw an old friend.*
- *We **were cleaning** the house all morning.* (extended duration)

**Critical distinction — same time word, different meaning:**

| Sentence | Meaning |
|----------|---------|
| *When the guests arrived, Jane **was cooking** dinner.* | Jane started cooking BEFORE guests arrived |
| *When the guests arrived, Jane **cooked** dinner.* | Guests arrived FIRST, then Jane started cooking |

**Key contrast:**

| Tense | Meaning | Example |
|-------|---------|---------|
| Past simple | Completed, finished action | *I read a book last night.* |
| Past continuous | Action in progress / background | *I was reading when she arrived.* |

## 17. PRESENT SIMPLE: HAVE GOT

`Have got` = possession; same meaning as `have` but more common in British English (speaking/informal).

**Structure:**

| | Positive | Negative | Question |
|-|----------|----------|----------|
| I / You / We / They | have got (I**'ve** got) | have**n't** got | **Have** you got...? |
| He / She / It | has got (She**'s** got) | has**n't** got | **Has** she got...? |

**Name contraction:** `My sister **'s got** long hair.` (sister's = sister has)

**Uses beyond simple possession:**
- Appearance: *I**'ve got** brown hair.*
- Timetabled events: *They**'ve got** basketball practice this evening.*
- Illnesses: *He**'s got** a terrible cold.*
- Abstract: *I**'ve got** a good idea!*

**Short answers:**
- *Have you got a car? — Yes, I **have**. / No, I **haven't**.*
- *Has she got a meeting? — Yes, she **has**. / No, she **hasn't**.*

**Note:** In questions and negatives, use **have/has** — NOT *do/does*:
- ✗ *Do you have got a car?*
- ✓ *Have you got a car?*

**Past tense:** use `had` — NOT "had got":
- ✗ *I had got a red bike.*
- ✓ *I had a red bike when I was young.*

**Have (without got)** — also correct, more common in American English:
- *I have a big family. / She has a car.*

## 18. QUANTIFIERS: FEW / A FEW / LITTLE / A BIT OF

The presence or absence of the article **a** changes the meaning significantly.

### Countable nouns → few / a few

| Quantifier | Meaning | Connotation | Example |
|------------|---------|-------------|---------|
| **a few** | some, a small number | **Positive** — enough | *I have **a few** friends, so I'm not lonely.* |
| **few** | barely any, hardly any | **Negative** — not enough | *She has **few** friends, so she's quite lonely.* |

### Uncountable nouns → little / a little / a bit of / very little

| Quantifier | Meaning | Connotation | Example |
|------------|---------|-------------|---------|
| **a bit of** | some, a small amount (informal) | **Positive** — enough | *We've got **a bit of** time. Shall we get coffee?* |
| **a little** | some, a small amount (neutral/formal) | **Positive** — enough | *It needs **a little** more work.* |
| **very little** | barely any | **Negative** — not enough | *We've got **very little** time. Hurry up!* |
| **little** (formal, rare) | barely any | **Negative** | *She had **little** water.* |

**Summary:**

| | With article (**a**) = positive | Without article = negative |
|-|-------------------------------|---------------------------|
| Countable | **a few** (some, enough) | **few** (barely any) |
| Uncountable | **a bit of** / **a little** | **(very) little** (barely any) |

## 19. QUESTION FORMS

### Inversion principle
To make a question, the **auxiliary verb** (or to be) moves **before the subject**:

| Affirmative | Question |
|-------------|---------|
| *I am late.* | ***Am** I late?* |
| *She is sleeping.* | ***Is** she sleeping?* |
| *I can help.* | ***Can** I help?* |
| *We have met.* | ***Have** we met?* |

Works for: **to be**, **auxiliary verbs** (is/are/was/were, have/has/had, will/would), and **modal verbs** (can, could, will, should, might, must).

**If there is a question word, it goes before the verb:**
- *Are you late?* → *Why **are** you late?*
- *Can I help?* → *How **can** I help?*
- *Have we met?* → *Where **have** we met?*

### Present simple and past simple — use do/does/did

For main verbs (not to be), add the auxiliary:

| Affirmative | Yes/No question | Wh- question |
|-------------|----------------|-------------|
| *You work at home.* | *Do you work at home?* | *Where **do** you work?* |
| *It costs £10.* | *Does it cost £10?* | *How much **does** it cost?* |
| *She went home.* | *Did she go home?* | *Where **did** she go?* |

### Wh- questions (information questions)

**Structure:** Wh- word + auxiliary + subject + main verb

| Wh- word | Asks about | Example |
|----------|-----------|---------|
| **What** | thing / action | *What do you do?* |
| **Where** | place | *Where do you live?* |
| **When** | time | *When did you get here?* |
| **Why** | reason | *Why does she look sad?* |
| **Who** | person (object) | *Who did you call?* |
| **Which** | choice | *Which one do you prefer?* |
| **How** | manner / degree | *How do you feel?* |
| **How much** | quantity (uncountable) | *How much does a ticket cost?* |
| **How many** | quantity (countable) | *How many people came?* |
| **How long** | duration | *How long have you lived here?* |

### Subject questions — no auxiliary needed

When **who** or **what** is the **subject** (doing the action), the word order is the same as a positive sentence — no *do/does/did*:

| Type | Example |
|------|---------|
| Normal (object) question | *Who did you call?* (you = subject) |
| Subject question | *Who called you?* (who = subject — no "did") |
| Normal question | *What did he say?* |
| Subject question | *What happened?* (what = subject — no "did") |

## 20. THERE IS / THERE ARE

Used to say that something **exists** or is **present** somewhere.

### Structure

| | Singular / Uncountable | Plural |
|-|----------------------|--------|
| **Positive** | There **is** (there**'s**) a park. | There **are** some restaurants. |
| **Negative** | There **isn't** a market. / There is **no** market. | There **aren't** any restaurants. / There are **no** restaurants. |
| **Question** | **Is there** a café? | **Are there** any seats? |
| **Short answer** | Yes, there is. / No, there isn't. | Yes, there are. / No, there aren't. |

**Examples:**
- *There's a very big park in my city.*
- *There aren't any street markets.*
- *There are no restaurants in the station.*
- *Is there a bank nearby? — Yes, there's one on the corner.*

### Informal spoken — lists
When listing things in speech, *there's* is often used even before a plural list:
- *There's a café, a supermarket and a bus stop on my street.* (informal)

### Negative patterns

| Pattern | Use | Example |
|---------|-----|---------|
| `there isn't a` | singular countable | *There isn't a café near here.* |
| `there isn't any` | uncountable | *There isn't any milk.* |
| `there aren't any` | plural countable | *There aren't any toilets.* |
| `there is no` | uncountable (emphasis) | *There's no milk.* |
| `there are no` | plural (emphasis) | *There are no toilets in the park.* |

### Other tenses
The structure works in any tense:
- *There **was** a storm last night.* (past simple)
- *There **were** a lot of cars.* (past simple)
- *There **will be** a lot of people tomorrow.* (future)

### Common mistake — do NOT use "it is" for existence:
- ✗ *It's a park near my house.*
- ✓ *There's a park near my house.*

---

## 21. VERBS FOLLOWED BY -ING OR INFINITIVE

### Verbs followed by **-ing** (gerund)

The verb directly follows without *to*; the -ing form acts as a noun.

Common verbs: `enjoy, finish, mind, avoid, can't help, consider, dislike, feel like, give up, keep, miss, practise, suggest, admit, deny`

- *I **enjoy learning** languages.*
- *She **finished writing** the report.*
- *Do you **mind opening** the window?*
- *He **keeps forgetting** his keys.*

### Verbs followed by **to + infinitive**

Common verbs: `want, need, ask, afford, agree, choose, decide, expect, hope, learn, manage, offer, plan, prepare, promise, refuse, would like`

- *I **want to learn** a new language.*
- *She **decided to leave** early.*
- *He **refused to answer** the question.*
- *We **hope to see** you soon.*

### Verbs that take **both** (little or no meaning difference)

`like, love, hate, prefer, begin, start, continue, intend`

- *I **like swimming**. / I **like to swim**.* (both correct)
- *It **started raining**. / It **started to rain**.* (both correct)

### Verbs that take **both** — with meaning difference

| Verb | + -ing | + to infinitive |
|------|--------|-----------------|
| **remember** | *I remember **locking** the door.* (memory of a past action) | *Remember **to lock** the door.* (future reminder) |
| **forget** | *I'll never forget **meeting** her.* (memory of past) | *I forgot **to call** him.* (failed future task) |
| **stop** | *She stopped **smoking**.* (quit the habit) | *She stopped **to smoke**.* (paused in order to smoke) |
| **try** | *Try **adding** more salt.* (experiment) | *I tried **to open** the window.* (attempt) |

---

## 22. ADJECTIVES: GRADABLE AND NON-GRADABLE

### Gradable adjectives
Can exist on a scale; modified by degree adverbs: `a bit, quite, very, really, extremely`

- *It's **a bit** cold. / **very** cold. / **extremely** cold.*
- *The exercise is **quite** difficult. / **really** difficult.*

### Non-gradable: absolute adjectives
Describe something that is complete/total — cannot exist on a scale. Use `absolutely, totally, completely` — **NOT** *very*.

Common absolute adjectives: `acceptable, dead, destroyed, finished, free, impossible, necessary, perfect, ruined, unacceptable`

- *My work is **completely finished**.*
- *Their farm was **totally destroyed** by a tornado.*
- *Thank you, it's **absolutely perfect**!*

### Non-gradable: extreme adjectives
Already contain the idea of "very" in their meaning. Use `absolutely` or `really` — **NOT** *very*, *completely*, or *totally*.

Common extreme adjectives: `amazing, ancient, awful, boiling, delicious, enormous, excellent, exhausted, fascinating, freezing, gorgeous, terrible, terrifying, tiny`

| Gradable | Extreme equivalent |
|----------|--------------------|
| cold | freezing |
| hot | boiling |
| good | excellent |
| bad | terrible / awful |
| big | enormous |
| tired | exhausted |
| surprised | amazed / astonished |
| dirty | filthy |

| ✗ Wrong | ✓ Correct |
|---------|-----------|
| *very freezing* | ***absolutely** freezing* |
| *completely exhausted* (for extreme) | ***absolutely** exhausted* |
| *very impossible* | ***completely** impossible* (absolute ✓) |

**Note:** `really` can be used with ALL categories:
- *It's **really** cold.* ✓ (gradable) / *It's **really** freezing.* ✓ (extreme) / *It's **really** impossible.* ✓ (absolute)

## 24. CONDITIONALS

**Clause order:** The if-clause can come first or second — meaning is the same:
- *If I hadn't been ill, I would have gone to the party.*
- *I would have gone to the party if I hadn't been ill.*

### Zero conditional — general truths and facts
`If/When + present simple, present simple`

Used when the result is **always** true. `When` can replace `if` here.
- *If you **freeze** water, it **becomes** solid.*
- ***When** the sun goes down, it **gets** dark.*

### First conditional — real future possibility
`If + present simple, will + infinitive`

Used for situations that are **likely** to happen.
- *If it **rains** tomorrow, I**'ll take** the car.*

**Alternatives to "if":** `unless, as long as, as soon as, in case`
- *I**'ll leave** as soon as the babysitter **arrives**.*
- *I don't want to stay unless I **get** a well-paid job.*
- *I**'ll give** you a key in case I**'m** not at home.*
- *You can go, as long as you**'re** back by midnight.*

### Second conditional — unreal present or future
`If + past simple, would + infinitive`

Used for **hypothetical** or **unlikely** situations.
- *If I **lived** closer, I **would go** more often.*
- *I **would buy** a boat if I **had** enough money.*

**were vs was:** Formally, use `were` for all subjects with `if + to be`. Informally, `was` is also common for he/she/it:
- *If I **were** you, I wouldn't mention it.* (formal ✓)
- *If she **was** prime minister, she would invest more in schools.* (informal, common)

### Third conditional — unreal past
`If + past perfect, would have + past participle`

Used to imagine a **different past** outcome.
- *We **would have walked** to the top if the weather **hadn't been** so bad.*
- *If she **had studied** harder, she **would have passed**.*

### Mixed conditionals

**Type 1 — Past cause, present result:** `If + past perfect >> would + infinitive`
- *If we**'d moved** to Scotland when I was a child, I **would have** a Scottish accent **now**.*

**Type 2 — Present condition, past result:** `If + past simple >> would have + past participle`
- *It's really important. If it **wasn't**, I **wouldn't have called** you on your holiday.*

### Summary table

| Type | If-clause | Main clause | Use |
|------|-----------|-------------|-----|
| Zero | present simple | present simple | Always true |
| First | present simple | will + inf | Real possibility |
| Second | past simple | would + inf | Unreal present/future |
| Third | past perfect | would have + pp | Unreal past |
| Mixed 1 | past perfect | would + inf | Past → present effect |
| Mixed 2 | past simple | would have + pp | Present → past effect |

## 25. CONTRASTING IDEAS

### Although / Even though + clause
Followed by a **full clause** (subject + verb). Interchangeable; *even though* is slightly stronger.

- ***Although** we don't agree, I think she's brilliant.*
- ***Even though** it was raining, we went for a walk.*
- Position flexible: clause can come first or second.

### Despite / In spite of + noun, pronoun or -ing form
**NOT** followed by a clause — must use a noun, gerund, or pronoun.

| ✗ Wrong | ✓ Correct |
|---------|-----------|
| *Despite we were tired…* | *Despite **being** tired…* |
| *In spite of it was raining…* | *In spite of **the rain**…* |

- ***Despite the law**, people still use phones while driving.*
- ***In spite of** feeling ill, she went to work.*

**Exception — + "the fact that" + clause:** Use this to add a full clause after despite/in spite of:
- *In spite of **the fact that** he worked very hard, he didn't pass.*
- *Despite **the fact that** she was qualified, she didn't get the job.*

### However — formal contrast
Placed at the beginning of a new sentence, followed by a comma.
- *It was expensive. **However**, it was worth it.*

### Though — informal contrast
Can go at the **end** of a sentence or at the **start** like *although*:
- *It's illegal. People still do it, **though**.* (end — most common in speech)
- ***Though** I wasn't keen on the film, I thought the music was beautiful.* (start)

### Even if — whether or not / no matter whether
Followed by a subject + verb. Describes a condition that doesn't change the result:
- ***Even if** you are an expert swimmer, you should be careful at this beach.*

### While / Whereas — comparing contradictory ideas
Used to mean "but" or "compared with the fact that." Can start the sentence or come between the two clauses:
- ***While** Ivan is very sociable, his brother is more reserved.*
- *My trip home was quick and easy, **whereas** my colleagues were delayed for hours.*

**While also means "although" at the start of a sentence:**
- ***While** I made some mistakes in my driving exam, I still passed.*

### Much as — formal, despite how much / although
- ***Much as** I'd love a holiday this year, I just can't afford it.*

## 26. PAST HABITS: USED TO, WOULD AND PAST SIMPLE

Three ways to talk about past habits/states no longer true:

### used to + infinitive — past habit (no longer true)
Something happened regularly in the past but **doesn't happen now**.
- *I **used to want** to be a lawyer.*
- *She **used to live** in Paris.* (she doesn't live there now)
- **Negative:** *I **didn't use to** like coffee.* (NOT *usedn't to*)
- **Question:** ***Did** you **use to** play football?*

### be used to — already accustomed
Followed by **noun, pronoun, or -ing form**. Can be used in any tense.
- *Are you **used to** the cold weather **yet**?* (present — adjective)
- *I'm **used to** working long hours.* (-ing)
- *He **wasn't used to** walking so much.* (past)
- *I'm a teacher, so I'm **used to it**.* (pronoun)

### get used to — becoming accustomed
Followed by **noun, pronoun, or -ing form**. Can be used in any tense.
- *I**'ll never get used to** take-off and landing.* (future)
- *I**'m getting used to** the noise.* (present continuous = in progress)
- *It took years to **get used to** living in London.* (past infinitive)

### would — repeated past actions (more formal, NOT states)
Use `would` for repeated past actions that no longer happen. Common in narratives. **Cannot be used for past states.**
- *Every Saturday I **would go** on a long bike ride.*
- *My dad **would read** me stories every night at bedtime.*
- ✗ *I would live in Paris.* (state — use "used to" instead)
- ✗ Negative/question forms of `would` are NOT used for past habits.

### Past simple — always an alternative
Can always replace `used to` or `would`, but:
- Does **not** emphasise repetition or that the habit has ended.
- **Must** be used for single past events (used to / would imply repetition).

| Structure | Use | Emphasises repetition? | Can describe states? |
|-----------|-----|----------------------|---------------------|
| **used to** + inf | Past habit or state, no longer true | ✓ Yes | ✓ Yes |
| **would** + inf | Repeated past action, no longer true | ✓ Yes | ✗ No |
| **Past simple** | Any past event (once or repeated) | ✗ Not specifically | ✓ Yes |

- *We went to the same beach every summer.* (past simple)
- *We **used to go** to the same beach every summer.* (emphasises habit, now ended)
- *We **would go** to the same beach every summer.* (same meaning, more formal)
- *I went to Egypt in 2014.* (once only → must use past simple)

## 27. FUTURE CONTINUOUS AND FUTURE PERFECT

### Future continuous — action in progress at a future time
`will be + verb(-ing)`

**Two uses:**

1. Action **in progress** at a specific future moment:
   - *In three years' time, I**'ll be studying** medicine.*
   - *This time tomorrow, I**'ll be flying** to Tokyo.*
   - *Don't call at 8 — I**'ll be having** dinner.*
   - *You can visit in July. I **won't be working** then.*

2. Action that is **new, different, or temporary**:
   - *Today we're taking the bus but next week we**'ll be taking** the train.*
   - *He**'ll be staying** with his parents while his father is in recovery.*

### Future perfect — action completed before a future point
`will have + past participle`

Used for an action that will be **completed** by a specific future time.
- *In five years' time, I**'ll have finished** studying.*
- *By the time you arrive, I**'ll have cooked** dinner.*
- *I **won't have written** all the reports by next week.*
- *By the time we arrive, the kids **will have gone** to bed.*
- *On 9 October we**'ll have been married** for 50 years.* (duration completed — future perfect + been)

### Contrast

| Tense | Focus | Example |
|-------|-------|---------|
| Future continuous | In progress at future point | *At 9pm, I'll be watching the match.* |
| Future perfect | Completed by future point | *By 9pm, I'll have finished work.* |

**Time markers:**
- Future continuous: *this time tomorrow, at [time], in [period]'s time*
- Future perfect: *by [time], by the time…, in [period]'s time*

## 28. CAPITAL LETTERS — COMPLETE RULES

### Always capitalise:
1. **First word of a sentence** and the pronoun **I**
2. **Days and months:** *Monday, Thursday, January, August*
   - ⚠ Seasons are **NOT** capitalised: *summer, winter, spring, autumn*
3. **Festivals and holidays:** *Christmas, Easter, Ramadan, Chinese New Year*
4. **Names of people and places** (streets, cities, countries, continents, planets):
   - *Bea Jankowski, Church Street, Manchester, Russia, Asia, the Earth, the Sun*
5. **Languages, nationalities, and adjectives from place names:**
   - *French, Canadian, Londoners, Indian food, American English*
6. **Nouns and adjectives from religions:**
   - *Muslim, Buddhist, Christian, Islamic, Hindu*
7. **Organisation names and important words in book/film titles:**
   - *the United Nations, War and Peace, The Lion King*
8. **Titles before a name:** *President Alvi, Ms Ioana, Dr Smith*
   - ⚠ Title used **without** a name → only the name gets capital: *Salome Zourabichvili, the president of Georgia, is visiting President Alvi.*
   - Job titles alone are NOT capitalised: *The chief executive officer lives in New York.*

## 29. FUTURE FORMS: WILL / BE GOING TO / PRESENT CONTINUOUS

Three different forms for future — choice depends on the **type of plan**.

### will — spontaneous decisions (decided at moment of speaking)
- *Oops, I forgot to phone Mum! I**'ll** do it after dinner.*
- *There's no milk. I**'ll** buy some when I go to the shops.*
- *I can't decide what to wear. I know — I**'ll** wear my green shirt.*

### be going to — pre-decided plans (decided before the moment of speaking)
- *I**'m going to** phone Mum after dinner. I told her I'd call at 8.*
- *I**'m going to** go to the supermarket after work. What do we need?*
- *I**'m going to** wear my black dress tonight.*

**Also:** `going to` for predictions based on present evidence:
- *Look at those clouds — it**'s going to** rain.*

### Present continuous — fixed arrangements (confirmed with others, time and place known)
- *I**'m meeting** Jane at 8 o'clock on Saturday.*
- *We**'re having** a party next Saturday. Would you like to come?*

**Asking about plans:** often use present continuous:
- *Are you **doing** anything interesting this weekend?*

### Summary

| Form | Type | Example |
|------|------|---------|
| **will** | Spontaneous decision | *I'll call her now.* |
| **going to** | Pre-decided plan | *I'm going to call her tonight.* |
| **Present continuous** | Fixed arrangement | *I'm calling her at 8 — we arranged it.* |

## 30. INTENSIFIERS: SO AND SUCH

Both make meaning stronger but used with different grammatical structures.

### so + adjective or adverb
- *It's **so** hot today!*
- *She looks **so** young in that photo.*
- *He walks **so** slowly.*

**so much** with comparatives:
- *They were **so much** more innocent when they were younger.*
- *I work **so much** more quickly when I can concentrate.*

**so + many/much/few/little** before nouns:
- *There are **so many** people here!*
- *I've had **so little** time to myself this week.*

### such + (a/an) + (adjective) + noun
- *You're **such** an angel!*
- *It's **such** a hot day today!*
- *They're **such** lovely trousers.*

### Result clauses with "that"
Both `so` and `such` can be followed by `that + result clause`:
- *It was **so cold that** the water in the lake froze.*
- *He was **such a good teacher that** we all passed the exam.*
- *There's **so much noise that** I can't think!*

### Quick rule

| Structure | Use |
|-----------|-----|
| **so** + adj/adv | *so hot, so quickly* |
| **so** + many/much/few/little + noun | *so many people, so much money* |
| **such** + a/an + adj + noun | *such a hot day, such an interesting book* |
| **such** + adj + plural/uncountable noun | *such lovely trousers, such good advice* |

## 31. MODALS: DEDUCTIONS ABOUT THE PRESENT

Used to guess how certain or likely something is right now.

### must — very certain (positive deduction)
Used when something is the **only realistic conclusion** from the evidence.
- *This must be her house. I can see her car in the garage.*
- *Come inside! You **must be** freezing out there!*
- *He **must live** near here — he always walks to work.*

### might / may / could — possible but not certain
All three express the same idea; `may` is more formal.
- *She's not here. She **might be** stuck in traffic.*
- *He's not answering. He **could be** in class.*
- *Some services **may be** delayed due to bad weather.* (formal)

### can't — very certain (negative deduction)
Used when something is **impossible** given the evidence.
- *It **can't be** far now. We've been driving for hours.*
- *She **can't know** about the complaint — she's just promoted him.*
- *You **can't be** bored already! You've only been here five minutes.*

### should / shouldn't — expectations (if all goes well)
Used to show we think something will **probably** happen because it is normal or reasonable:
- *Rest and drink water. You **should feel** better in a day or two.*
- *It **shouldn't be** a problem.*
- ⚠ NOT used for unwanted outcomes: ✗ *The treatment should be very painful.*

### be bound to — very certain (prediction/guess)
- *There are transport strikes tomorrow, so travel **is bound to be** more difficult.*
- *They**'re bound to** win — they're by far the best team.*

### might well / may well / could well — more probable than might/may/could
Adding `well` after the modal makes the situation sound **more likely**:
- *I'll try the pharmacy, but it **might well be** closed now.*
- *She **may well** have to rethink her plan.*
- *That **could well be** true.*

### can — general possibility (not specific events)
`can` does NOT express possibility about a specific event. It expresses what **sometimes happens** or is **capable of happening** in general:
- ✗ *Azi can be in the garden.* (specific moment — wrong)
- ✓ *Noisy neighbours **can** be a problem in flats.* (general/sometimes)
- ✓ *It **can** be very cold here in winter.* (general pattern)

### Summary

| Modal | Certainty | Example |
|-------|----------|---------|
| **must** | Very sure it IS true | *He must be tired.* |
| **be bound to** | Very sure (prediction) | *She's bound to win.* |
| **should** | Expected/probable | *It should be fine.* |
| **might well/may well** | More likely (possible) | *It might well be closed.* |
| **might/may/could** | Possible, not sure | *She might be at home.* |
| **can't** | Very sure it is NOT true | *It can't be him — he's abroad.* |

⚠ All modal verbs are followed by **infinitive without to**: *She must **be** here. / It can't **be** true.*

---

## 32. MODALS: DEDUCTIONS ABOUT THE PAST

Same logic as present deductions, but add **have + past participle** after the modal.

### must have — very certain (positive, past)
- *Who told the newspapers? It **must have been** someone close to him.*
- *The thief **must have had** a key. The door was locked.*
- *Oh, we've got milk — Mo **must have bought** some yesterday.*

### might have / may have / could have — possible (past)
- *I think I **might have left** the air conditioning on.*
- *Police think the suspect **may have left** the country using a fake passport.*
- *She **could have taken** the wrong train.*

`May have` is more formal than `might have`. `Could have` is also possible but less common in this context.

### can't have / couldn't have — very certain (negative, past)
- *She **can't have driven** there. Her car keys are still here.*
- *I thought I saw Adnan but it **couldn't have been** him — he's in Greece.*
- *They **can't have known** about the meeting. No one told them.*

### Summary

| Modal | Certainty | Structure | Example |
|-------|----------|-----------|---------|
| **must have** + pp | Sure it happened | must have + pp | *She must have left.* |
| **might/may/could have** + pp | Possibly happened | modal + have + pp | *He might have missed it.* |
| **can't/couldn't have** + pp | Impossible | can't/couldn't + have + pp | *It can't have been her.* |

## 33. MODALS: PERMISSION AND OBLIGATION

### Permission — can / could / may

| Modal | Use | Formality | Example |
|-------|-----|----------|---------|
| **can** | Ask for / give permission | Informal | *Can I sit here? / You can use my car.* |
| **could** | Ask for permission only (NOT give) | More polite | *Could I borrow your pen?* |
| **may** | Ask for / give permission | Most formal | *May I see your passport? / Pages may be photocopied.* |

### Prohibition — can't / mustn't

| Modal | Use | Example |
|-------|-----|---------|
| **can't** | Against rules (rules not made by speaker) | *You can't take photos in the museum.* |
| **mustn't / must not** | Not permitted — public rules/signs | *Baggage must not be left unattended.* |
| **mustn't** | Prohibition from speaker personally | *(Parent) You mustn't say things like that.* |

### Obligation — have to / must

| Modal | Source of obligation | Example |
|-------|---------------------|---------|
| **have to** | External — comes from outside the speaker | *We have to wear a uniform. / I had to pay £85.* |
| **must** | Internal — strong personal obligation or authority | *I must phone my dad. / Seat belts must be worn.* |

**Past obligation:** use `had to` — NOT `must`:
- ✗ *I must renew my passport last week.*
- ✓ *I **had to** pay £85 to renew my passport last week.*

### No obligation — don't have to

Something is **not compulsory** — it's optional:
- *You **don't have to** wear a tie, but some people like to.*
- *You **don't have to** go to the bank — you can do it online.*

### Summary

| Modal | Meaning |
|-------|---------|
| can / could / may | Permission (can = give + ask; could/may = ask) |
| can't | Prohibited (external rules) |
| mustn't | Prohibited (personal/authority) |
| must | Strong obligation (internal/authority) |
| have to | Obligation (external) |
| had to | Past obligation |
| don't have to | No obligation (optional) |

## 34. MODIFYING COMPARATIVES

### Basic comparison forms

| Form | Use | Example |
|------|-----|---------|
| comparative + **than** | Two things differ | *Max is taller than Judy.* |
| **as … as** | Two things are equal | *Her hair is as long as mine.* |
| **not as … as** | One is less than the other | *It's not as sunny as yesterday.* |
| **different from** | Things differ | *England is different from the UK.* |
| **similar to** | Things are alike | *His car is similar to mine.* |
| **the same as** | Things are identical | *The results are the same as before.* |

### Showing big differences

| Modifier | Used with | Example |
|----------|-----------|---------|
| **much, a lot, far, even, so much** | comparative | *He's **far** less experienced. / Sales were **a lot** higher.* |
| **nowhere near** | as … as | *The interview was **nowhere near** as hard as the exam.* |
| **very, really, completely, totally** | different from | *They're **completely** different from each other.* |

### Showing small differences

| Modifier | Used with | Example |
|----------|-----------|---------|
| **slightly, a little, a bit, not much** | comparative | *Houses are **slightly** more expensive.* |
| **almost, nearly, not quite, roughly, more or less, about** | as…as / the same as | *She's **almost** as old as I am. / The figures are **more or less** the same.* |
| **very, really** | similar to | *My son looks **really** similar to my father.* |

### Showing no difference

- ***exactly** the same as*: *My grandma's cakes still taste **exactly the same as** when I was a child.*
- ***just** as … as*: *A new phone can be **just as** expensive as a new computer.*

## 35. PASSIVE VOICE

### Why use the passive?
- When the agent (doer) is unknown, obvious, or unimportant to mention.
- To put the most important information first (the object becomes subject).
- In formal, scientific, or news writing.

*My bike was stolen.* (passive — focus on bike, agent unknown)
*Someone stole my bike.* (active — focus on the person)

### Structure: `be + past participle`

| Tense | Structure | Example |
|-------|-----------|---------|
| Present simple | is/are + pp | *Alioli **is made** from oil, garlic and salt.* |
| Present continuous | is/are being + pp | *The hall **is being painted** this week.* |
| Past simple | was/were + pp | *Kennedy **was assassinated** in 1963.* |
| Past continuous | was/were being + pp | *Signs **were being put up** last week.* |
| Present perfect | has/have been + pp | *Oranges **have been grown** here for centuries.* |
| Past perfect | had been + pp | *His flat **had been burgled**.* |
| Future simple | will be + pp | *The work **will be finished** next week.* |

**Adding the agent:** use `by`:
- *Avatar was directed **by** James Cameron.*
- Not always necessary: *My flight is cancelled.*

### Modal + passive: `modal + be + past participle`
- *A podcast **can be made** with minimal resources.*
- *The accident **must be reported** to the police.*
- *New laws **should be created** to regulate electric scooters.*

### Get passive (informal): `get + past participle`
Same meaning as `be + pp` but more informal, often for sudden/negative events:
- *My bicycle **got stolen** last night.* (= was stolen)
- *She **got promoted** last week.*

### Impersonal passive — reporting verbs
Used with verbs like `allege, believe, claim, consider, estimate, expect, know, report, say, think, understand` to report what an unspecified group believes. Two equivalent patterns:

**Pattern 1:** `It + be + past participle + (that) + clause`
- *It **is estimated** that millions of people visit the site every year.*
- *It **is believed** that the walls date from the third century BCE.*

**Pattern 2:** `Subject + be + past participle + infinitive`
- *Millions of people **are estimated** to visit the site every year.*
- *The walls **are believed** to date from the third century BCE.*

The infinitive can be simple, perfect (past action), or continuous (in progress):
- *simple:* *Mosquitoes are reported **to transmit** the disease.*
- *perfect:* *The walls are believed **to have been built** in the third century BCE.*
- *continuous:* *Mosquitoes are reported **to be transmitting** the disease.*

## 36. PAST ABILITY

### General ability — could / couldn't
Use for **repeated** or **general** ability in the past:
- *She **could** paint before she started school.*
- *I **couldn't** cook until I went to university.*

### Specific occasion — successful — was/were able to / managed to
Use for achieving something on **one specific occasion**. `managed to` implies extra difficulty.
- *The burglar **was able to** get in through the window.*
- *The burglar **managed to** get in even though it was locked.*
- ✗ *The burglar could get in.* (NOT correct for a single specific occasion)

### Specific occasion — unsuccessful — wasn't/weren't able to / couldn't / didn't manage to
All three are correct for a specific failure:
- *The speaker **wasn't able to** attend due to illness.* (formal)
- *She **couldn't** watch the match because she was working.*
- *They **didn't manage to** find a solution.* (emphasises difficulty)

### Summary

| | General ability | Specific success | Specific failure |
|-|----------------|-----------------|-----------------|
| ✓ Use | could/couldn't | was/were able to, managed to | couldn't, wasn't able to, didn't manage to |
| ✗ Avoid | — | ~~could~~ | — |

## 37. PAST PERFECT

`had + past participle`

### Time up to a point in the past
Used to describe something completed **before** a certain past moment:
- *She**'d** published her first poem by the time she was eight.*
- *We**'d** finished all the water before we were halfway up the mountain.*
- *Had the parcel arrived when you called yesterday?*

### Sequencing two past events
Past perfect = **earlier** action; past simple = **later** action. Order in the sentence doesn't matter — the tenses show the sequence:
- *When the police arrived, the thief **had escaped**.* (thief left first)
- *The thief **had escaped** when the police arrived.* (same meaning)

**Single event — don't use past perfect:**
- ✓ *The Romans spoke Latin.* (single historical fact)
- ✗ *The Romans had spoken Latin.*

### Before + past perfect
Shows an action was **not done** or incomplete when the past simple action happened:
- *They left before I**'d spoken** to them.*
- *The author died before he**'d finished** the series.*

### Key adverbs with past perfect

| Adverb | Meaning | Example |
|--------|---------|---------|
| **already** | before the specified time | *He**'d already left** when I called.* |
| **still** | as previously (negative) | *It **still hadn't rained** in May.* |
| **just** | a very short time before | *She**'d just moved** to Berlin when I visited.* |
| **ever** | at any time before | *It was the most beautiful photo I**'d ever seen**.* |
| **never** | at no time before | *I**'d never met** anyone from California before.* |

## 38. PHRASAL VERBS

Phrasal verbs = verb + particle (preposition or adverb). The particle often changes meaning significantly.

### Separable phrasal verbs
Verb and particle can be together or apart. **Must** be separated when using a personal pronoun:
- *They**'ve called** the meeting **off**.* ✓
- *They**'ve called off** the meeting.* ✓
- *They**'ve called it off**.* ✓ (pronoun → must separate)
- ✗ *They've called off it.*

**Word order preference:**
- If speaker **stresses the particle** → separation preferred in speech: *Take that rubbish **OUT**!*
- If object is **long or complex** → keep verb+particle together: *It's my job to sort out any kind of problem in our systems.* (not: *sort any kind of problem … out*)

| Phrasal verb | Meaning | Example |
|--------------|---------|---------|
| **bring up** | start talking about a subject | *I didn't want to bring the situation up.* |
| **fill in** | write information in a form | *Please can you fill this form in?* |
| **pick up** | collect someone in a vehicle | *I'll pick you up from the station.* |
| **turn down** | not accept an offer | *She turned the job down.* |

### Non-separable phrasal verbs
Verb and particle always stay together — even with pronouns:
- *Who looks after her?* ✓ (NOT: ✗ *Who looks after?*)

| Phrasal verb | Meaning | Example |
|--------------|---------|---------|
| **come across** | find by chance | *I came across your email.* |
| **turn into** | become | *The caterpillar turned into a butterfly.* |
| **get over** | recover from | *It took months to get over it.* |
| **look into** | investigate | *We are looking into the problem.* |

### Two-particle phrasal verbs (always inseparable)
Pronoun goes **after** both particles:
- *Who came up with that idea?* → *Who came up with it?*

| Phrasal verb | Meaning | Example |
|--------------|---------|---------|
| **come up with** | think of an idea | *We need to come up with a solution.* |
| **get rid of** | remove / free yourself of | *Let's get rid of these old magazines.* |
| **get on with** | like / be friendly with | *I didn't get on with my stepbrother.* |
| **put up with** | tolerate | *I don't know how you put up with it.* |
| **look forward to** | be excited about (future) | *I'm really looking forward to it.* |

## 39. PRESENT PERFECT

`have / has + past participle`

Used for past actions or states **still connected to the present**.

### Unfinished time periods
Events within a period that hasn't ended yet:
- *They**'ve been** on holiday twice **this year**.*
- *We **haven't had** positive feedback **so far**.*
- *I'm sure I**'ve seen** that film **before**.*

### Life experiences (life = unfinished period)
- *I**'ve worked** for six different companies.*
- *He**'s never won** a gold medal.*
- *Have you **ever been** to Australia?*

### Unfinished states — for / since / how long
- *She**'s wanted** to be a police officer **since** she was a child.*
- *How long **have** you **had** that phone?*

### Past action with present result
- *He**'s broken** his leg so he can't go on holiday.*
- *There**'s been** an accident — let's take another route.*

### When to use past simple instead
If we say **when** it happened, or the period is **finished**, use past simple:
- ✓ *We visited Russia **in 1992**.*
- ✓ *I went to three different primary schools.* (childhood = finished period)
- ✓ *He broke his leg **last week**.*

### Key adverbs with present perfect

| Adverb | Position | Example |
|--------|----------|---------|
| **just** | between have/has and pp | *I**'ve just seen** Susan.* |
| **recently** | between have/has and pp | *We**'ve recently started** the gym.* |
| **already** | between have/has and pp OR end | *She**'s already finished** season one.* |
| **yet** | end of sentence (negatives/questions) | *Have you finished **yet**? / I haven't finished **yet**.* |
| **still** | before haven't/hasn't (negatives) | *I **still haven't** called Yumi.* |
| **ever** | between have/has and pp (questions) | ***Have** you **ever** been to Australia?* |
| **never** | between have/has and pp (negatives) | *He**'s never** won a gold medal.* |

## 40. PRESENT PERFECT SIMPLE VS CONTINUOUS

Both talk about past actions connected to the present, but with different focus.

| | Simple (`have/has + pp`) | Continuous (`have/has been + -ing`) |
|-|--------------------------|--------------------------------------|
| **Focus** | Result of the activity | The activity itself |
| **Example** | *You**'ve cleaned** the bathroom!* | *I**'ve been gardening**.* |
| **Quantity** | Says **how many** | Says **how long** |
| **Example** | *She**'s read** ten books this summer.* | *She**'s been reading** all day.* |
| **Completion** | Completed action | May be ongoing |
| **Example** | *I**'ve written** you an email.* | *I**'ve been writing** emails.* |
| **Recent evidence** | — | *The grass is wet. Has it **been raining**?* |

### Ongoing states — simple (for/since/how long)
- *How long **have** you **known** each other?*
- *We**'ve known** each other since we were at school.*

### Ongoing repeated actions — continuous (for/since/how long)
- *How long **have** they **been playing** tennis?*
- *They**'ve been playing** tennis for an hour / every Sunday for years.*

### Temporary emphasis — continuous
- *I usually go to that gym, but it's closed so I**'ve been going** to the one in the shopping centre.*

## 41. PRESENT PERFECT: JUST, YET, STILL, ALREADY

| Word | Meaning | Position | Use in | Example |
|------|---------|----------|--------|---------|
| **just** | a short time before now | between have/has and pp | positives | *I**'ve just seen** Susan.* |
| **yet** | up to now (expecting it soon) | end of sentence | negatives & questions | *Have you finished **yet**? / I haven't finished **yet**.* |
| **still** | expected earlier (hasn't happened) | before haven't/hasn't | negatives only | *The bus **still hasn't** come.* |
| **already** | before now / earlier than expected | between have/has and pp OR end | positives | *I**'ve already** spent my salary. / The train's left **already**!* |

**Position summary:**
- `just` → *I've **just** seen*
- `yet` → *Have you finished **yet**?*
- `still` → *She **still** hasn't replied*
- `already` → *I've **already** eaten* OR *I've eaten **already***

## 42. QUESTION TAGS

A short question added to the end of a statement to confirm or check information.

### Core rule:
- Positive statement → **negative** tag
- Negative statement → **positive** tag

| Statement | Tag |
|-----------|-----|
| *She's a doctor,* | *isn't she?* |
| *Yesterday was fun,* | *wasn't it?* |
| *He isn't here,* | *is he?* |
| *The trains are never on time,* | *are they?* |
| *Nobody has called,* | *have they?* |

### Formation — use the auxiliary verb from the statement

| Statement | Tag |
|-----------|-----|
| *I don't need to finish today,* | *do I?* |
| *James is working on that,* | *isn't he?* |
| *Your parents have retired,* | *haven't they?* |
| *The phone didn't ring,* | *did it?* |
| *It was raining that day,* | *wasn't it?* |
| *Your mum hadn't met him,* | *had she?* |

### When there's no auxiliary — add do/does/did (present/past simple)

| Statement | Tag |
|-----------|-----|
| *Jenni eats cheese,* | *doesn't she?* |
| *I said that already,* | *didn't I?* |

### To be in present/past simple — use to be
| Statement | Tag |
|-----------|-----|
| *The bus stop's over there,* | *isn't it?* |
| *None of those customers were happy,* | *were they?* |

### Modal verbs — use the modal
| Statement | Tag |
|-----------|-----|
| *They could hear me,* | *couldn't they?* |
| *You won't tell anyone,* | *will you?* |

### Special case — am
| Statement | Tag |
|-----------|-----|
| *I'm never on time,* | *am I?* |
| *I'm going to get the details,* | ***aren't** I?* (negative of am I = aren't I) |

### Intonation
- **Falling** intonation → speaker is sure, just confirming
- **Rising** intonation → speaker is less certain, genuinely asking

## 43. REFLEXIVE PRONOUNS

| Subject pronoun | Reflexive pronoun |
|-----------------|------------------|
| I | **myself** |
| you (sg) | **yourself** |
| he | **himself** |
| she | **herself** |
| it | **itself** |
| we | **ourselves** |
| you (pl) | **yourselves** |
| they | **themselves** |

### Subject = object (main use)
- *I **cut myself** when I was making dinner.*
- *My phone **turns itself** off for no reason.*
- *We need to believe in **ourselves**.*

### Emphasis — doing it personally, not someone else
- *The door was definitely locked. I locked it **myself**.*
- *Are you redecorating your flat **yourselves**?*
- *We talked to the manager **herself**, and she agreed.*

### by + reflexive pronoun = alone
- *He usually goes on holiday **by himself**.*
- *Do you enjoy being **by yourself**?*

### Reflexive vs reciprocal pronouns (each other / one another)

| | Meaning | Example |
|-|---------|---------|
| **Reflexive** (themselves) | Each person acts on themselves | *They're buying **themselves** a new TV.* |
| **Reciprocal** (each other) | Each person acts on the other | *They're buying **each other** small gifts.* |
| **Reflexive** | Looking at own reflection | *We looked at **ourselves** in the mirror.* |
| **Reciprocal** | Looking at each other | *We looked at **each other** in surprise.* |

## 44. RELATIVE CLAUSES: DEFINING

Give **essential** information — tells us exactly who or what we mean. No commas.

### Relative pronouns

| Pronoun | Refers to | Notes |
|---------|-----------|-------|
| **who** | people | *She's the woman **who** cuts my hair.* |
| **that** | people or things | More common, slightly informal |
| **which** | things | *The laptop **which** I bought is broken.* |
| **when** | time | *Summer is the season **when** I'm happiest.* |
| **where** | place | *That's the stadium **where** Real Madrid play.* |
| **whose** | possession | *He's a musician **whose** albums have sold millions.* |

**who vs that (people):** both correct; `that` is more informal:
- *He's the man **that/who** I met at the conference.*

**which vs that (things):** both correct; `that` is more informal:
- *The laptop **that/which** I bought has started making a noise.*

### Omitting the relative pronoun
Can omit `who/which/that` when followed by a **subject** (not a verb):
- *The assistant [**that**] we met was really kind.* → can omit (we = subject follows)
- *The assistant **that** helped us was really kind.* → cannot omit (helped = verb follows)

---

## 45. RELATIVE CLAUSES: NON-DEFINING

Give **extra information** — not essential for identifying who/what. Always separated by **commas**.

**Cannot use `that`** — only `who, which, whose, when, where`.

### Who / which / whose
- *My grandfather, **who**'s 87, goes swimming every day.*
- *The house, **which** was built in 1883, has just been opened.*
- *My next-door neighbour, **whose** children go to school with ours, has just bought a car.*

### Places and times — which (with preposition) or where/when
- *City Park, **which we used to go to**, has been closed.* (with preposition)
- *City Park, **where** we used to go, has been closed.* (prefer where/when when preposition removed)
- *December, **when** Christmas is celebrated, is a summer month in the south.*

**But:** if `which` has no preposition, cannot substitute `where/when`:
- *Centre Park, **which** we love, is always busy on Saturdays.* (cannot use "where" here)

## 46. REPORTED SPEECH: STATEMENTS

When reporting what someone said, we usually shift the tense **"back"** (backshift) and change pronouns.

### Tense backshift

| Direct speech | Reported speech |
|---------------|----------------|
| Present simple | Past simple |
| Present continuous | Past continuous |
| Present perfect | Past perfect |
| Past simple | Past perfect |
| Past continuous | Past perfect continuous |
| Past perfect | Past perfect (no change) |
| will | would |
| can | could |

**Examples:**
- *"I travel a lot."* → She said that she **travelled** a lot.
- *"The baby's sleeping!"* → He told me the baby **was sleeping**.
- *"I've hurt my leg."* → She said she**'d hurt** her leg.
- *"We lived in China."* → She told me they**'d lived** in China.
- *"I'll call you."* → He said he**'d call** me.

### No backshift — when still true or relevant
If the reported fact is still true now, backshift is optional:
- *"I go to the gym next to your house."* → Jenny told me she **goes/went** to the gym there.

### Pronoun changes
- *"I enjoy working in **my** garden," said Bob.* → Bob said that **he** enjoyed working in **his** garden.
- Exception: if **you** are the one who spoke, pronouns don't change.

### Demonstratives, time and place changes

| Direct | Reported (if context changed) |
|--------|------------------------------|
| this | that |
| here | there |
| now | then |
| today | that day |
| yesterday | the day before |
| tomorrow | the next/following day |
| ago | before |

- *"I'm planning to do it **today**."* → She said she was planning to do it **that day**.
- *"**This** is my house."* → He said **that** was his house. (if no longer in front of it)

## 47. REPORTED SPEECH: QUESTIONS

### Yes/No questions → if / whether
- *"Are you going to the conference?"* → He asked me **if** I was going to the conference.
- *"Have you finished yet?"* → She asked **whether** we'd finished. (`whether` is more formal)

### Wh- questions → keep the question word; change to statement word order
- *"What time does the train leave?"* → He asked me **what time** the train **left**.
- *"Where did he go?"* → She asked **where** he **went**.

**⚠ Word order:** In reported questions, use **statement order** (subject + verb), NOT question order:
- ✗ *She asked where did he go.*
- ✓ *She asked where **he went**.*

### Reporting verbs for questions
`ask` (most common), `enquire`, `want to know`, `wonder`

### Offers, requests, suggestions → specific verb patterns
| Direct | Pattern | Reported |
|--------|---------|---------|
| *"Would you like me to help?"* | offer + infinitive | *He **offered to help** me.* |
| *"Can you hold this?"* | ask + obj + infinitive | *She **asked me to hold** it.* |
| *"Why don't we check with Joel?"* | suggest + -ing | *She **suggested checking** with Joel.* |

## 48. REPORTED SPEECH: REPORTING VERBS

### Reporting verb + to + infinitive
`advise, agree, challenge, claim, decide, demand, encourage, invite, offer, persuade, promise, refuse, remind`

- *"I'll do the report by Friday."* → She **promised to do** the report by Friday.
- *"It's not a good idea to write passwords down."* → They **advised us not to write** our passwords down.

**Imperatives** — use `tell, order, instruct, direct, warn` + object + infinitive:
- *"Please wait in reception."* → The guide **told us to wait**.
- *"Don't go in there!"* → The officer **warned us not to go** in there.

### Reporting verb + -ing form (directly after verb)
`admit, deny, mention, recommend, suggest`
- *She **admitted breaking** the window.*
- *She **suggested taking** a break.*

### Reporting verb + preposition + -ing
`apologise for, complain about, confess to, insist on, object to`; also `warn against`, `admit to`
- *He **apologised for** not getting back to me.*
- *Employees **complained about** receiving information too late.*
- *She **warns against** moving too fast.*

### Reporting verb + object + preposition + -ing
`accuse (sb of), blame (sb for), congratulate (sb on)`
- *They **accused the government of** lying about the issue.*
- *Parents **blame schools for** not doing enough.*

### Reporting verb + that + subjunctive (base form — no tense)
Used when the verb expresses what is **wanted or demanded**: `advise, ask, command, demand, insist, order, propose, recommend, request, suggest, urge`
- *We **suggest that** she **read** the documents carefully before signing.*
- *Steiner **demanded that** the government **investigate** the issue.*

### Reporting verb + that + indicative (any tense)
When NOT expressing a desire — just reporting: `admit, advise, agree, claim, complain, decide, deny, explain, insist, promise, recommend, say`
- *Customers **complained that** they **had not been kept** informed.*
- *Sengupta **agrees that** we need a better system.*

### Reporting verb + object + that clause
`assure, persuade, remind, tell, warn`
- *She **assured us that** they would fully investigate.*
- *Gairola **warned ministers that** the situation was critical.*

## 49. STATIVE VERBS (Extended)

*Basic rule → §3 (stative verbs cannot take -ing)*

Stative verbs describe a **state** (not an action) and are NOT used in continuous forms.

### Categories of stative verbs

| Category | Examples |
|----------|---------|
| Thoughts/opinions | `agree, believe, doubt, guess, imagine, know, mean, recognise, remember, suspect, think, understand` |
| Feelings/emotions | `dislike, hate, like, love, prefer, want, wish` |
| Senses/perceptions | `appear, be, feel, hear, look, see, seem, smell, taste` |
| Possession/measurement | `belong, have, measure, own, possess, weigh` |

### Verbs that can be BOTH stative AND dynamic

| Verb | Stative (state) | Dynamic (action) |
|------|-----------------|-----------------|
| **think** | *I **think** it's a good idea.* (opinion) | *I**'m thinking** about the problem.* (actively processing) |
| **have** | *I **have** an old car.* (possession) | *I**'m having** a quick break.* (activity) |
| **see** | *Do you **see** any problems?* (understand/opinion) | *We**'re seeing** Tadanari tomorrow.* (meeting) |
| **be** | *He**'s** so interesting!* (permanent quality) | *He**'s being** very unhelpful.* (temporary behaviour) |
| **taste** | *This coffee **tastes** delicious.* (perception) | *The chef **is tasting** the soup.* (activity) |
| **smell** | *It **smells** like coffee.* (perception) | *She **is smelling** the flowers.* (action) |
| **weigh** | *It **weighs** 3 kg.* (measurement) | *They **are weighing** the luggage.* (action) |
| **appear** | *She **appears** tired.* (seems) | *He**'s appearing** in a new film.* (performing) |

## 50. THE FUTURE: DEGREES OF CERTAINTY

### Modal verbs + adverbs

| Certainty | Positive | Negative |
|-----------|----------|---------|
| **Very sure** | *People **will definitely** work from home more.* | *Robots **definitely won't** replace all jobs.* |
| **Sure** | *Donna **will** really enjoy this.* | *You **won't** regret it.* |
| **Almost sure** | *We**'ll probably** finish by tomorrow.* | *He **probably won't** have time.* |
| **Not sure** | *I **might** go to the party.* | *He **might not** pass.* |

`may, could` also express "not sure" (positive); `may not` also possible. **Don't use `could not` for future uncertainty.**

### Other expressions

| Certainty | Expression | Example |
|-----------|-----------|---------|
| **Very sure** | be bound to | *He**'s bound to** feel nervous.* |
| **Very sure** | be certain to | *She**'s certain to** get the job!* |
| **Very sure** | there's no chance / no way | *There**'s no way** my boss will give me the day off.* |
| **Sure** | I'm sure that | *I**'m sure** you'll do well.* |
| **Almost sure** | be likely to | *The government**'s likely to** call an election.* |
| **Almost sure** | be unlikely to | *Ali**'s unlikely to** be invited.* |
| **Almost sure** | there's a good chance | *There**'s a good chance** it'll snow.* |
| **Almost sure** | I think / I don't think | *I **don't think** we'll have petrol cars in the future.* |
| **Almost sure** | I doubt | *I **doubt** they'll have trouble finding the address.* |
| **Not sure** | there's a chance | *There**'s a chance** she'll be back tomorrow.* |

## 51. AS AND LIKE

### Comparisons: as + adjective + as / as much as
- *She loves curry **as much as** I do.*
- *He**'s not as tall as** his brother.*
- *Your phone is **the same as** mine.* (use `as`, not `like`, with `the same`)

### Comparisons: like + noun/pronoun
- *He**'s like** a father to me.*
- *She**'s acting like** a child.*

**Like with sense verbs:**
- *She **looks like** her mother. / It **sounds like** a cat. / Nothing **tastes like** homemade lemonade.*

### as if / as though + clause (imaginary comparison)
- *You **look as if** you've seen a ghost.*
- *You **talk as though** we're never going to see each other again.*

### Giving examples: like / such as
- *You could try a team sport **like** football or basketball.*
- *You should take something soft, **such as** a towel.*

### as + noun = job or function
- *I worked **as a shop assistant** for two years.*
- *He used his coat **as a blanket** to keep warm.*

### as = conjunction (connecting phrases)

| Meaning | Example |
|---------|---------|
| **because** | *All the tickets were sold out **as** we got there too late.* |
| **while** | *She called **as** I was getting out of the bath.* |
| **in the way that** | ***As** you know, classes restart on 15 January.* |

*Informal speech: `like` is sometimes used for "in the way that" — Like I said, I didn't know her.*

## 52. ENOUGH

Means "as much as necessary."

### Position rules

| Used with | Position | Example |
|-----------|----------|---------|
| **Adjective** | AFTER the adjective | *I**'m not tall enough** to reach the shelf.* |
| **Adverb** | AFTER the adverb | *I couldn't write **quickly enough**.* |
| **Verb** | AFTER the verb | *I make sure I drink **enough** during the day.* |
| **Noun** | BEFORE the noun | *There isn**'t enough bread**.* |

### As a pronoun (without a noun)
- *I'll get some more chairs. There aren't **enough**.*
- *A: Do you want more coffee? B: No, I've had **enough**, thanks.*

### With adjective AND noun — position changes meaning
- *We haven't got **big enough** envelopes.* → the envelopes are too small
- *We haven't got **enough big** envelopes.* → we have some big ones, but not enough of them

### enough of + determiner/pronoun
- *There isn't **enough of that bread** to make sandwiches for everyone.*
- *I've seen **enough of his work** to recommend him.*

---

## 53. VERBS AND PREPOSITIONS

There are no rules — learn verb + preposition as fixed phrases.

### Verbs with **for**
`wait for, apologise for, apply for, ask for, prepare for, care for`
- *They're **waiting for** a bus.*
- *He **apologised for** being late.*
- *I **applied for** the job but didn't get it.*

### Verbs with **from**
`protect from, recover from, save from, suffer from`
- *This spray should **protect** you **from** mosquitoes.*
- *Has he **recovered from** the accident yet?*

### Verbs with **in**
`believe in, specialise in, succeed in`
- *She doesn't **believe in** coincidences.*
- *Our company **specialises in** software.*

### Verbs with **of**
`approve of, die of, smell of`
- *I don't **approve of** hunting animals for fur.*
- *This shampoo **smells of** bananas.*

### Verbs with **on**
`depend on, be based on, concentrate on, rely on, agree on`
- *Their decision will **depend on** the results.*
- *The film is **based on** a novel.*
- *We're **relying on** you!*

### Verbs with **to**
`listen to, introduce to, refer to, respond to, apologise to`
- *What music do you like **listening to**?*
- *Nobody **responded to** my complaint.*

### Verbs with **with**
`agree with, provide with, deal with`
- *I **agree with** everything you've said.*
- *We're finding it difficult to **deal with** the stress.*

## 54. WISHES: WISH AND IF ONLY

Both express a desire for something to be different. `If only` is slightly stronger/more emphatic.

### Wishing about the present → wish/if only + past simple
- *I **wish** you didn't live so far away.*
- ***If only** we knew what to do.*
- *He **wishes** he could afford a holiday.*

### Wishing about the past → wish/if only + past perfect
- *They **wish** they **hadn't eaten** so much chocolate.*
- ***If only** I**'d studied** harder at school.*

### Expressing annoyance → wish + would(n't)
Used when we want someone (or something) to stop or start doing something — often unlikely to change:
- *I **wish** you **wouldn't borrow** my clothes without asking.*
- *I **wish** it **would rain**. The garden really needs water.*
- *She **wishes** he**'d work** less. They never spend time together.*

### Summary

| Situation | Structure | Example |
|-----------|-----------|---------|
| Present unreal | wish/if only + past simple | *I wish I lived closer.* |
| Past unreal | wish/if only + past perfect | *If only I'd studied harder.* |
| Annoyed with habit | wish + would(n't) | *I wish you wouldn't do that.* |

*Note: wish + would is for **other people's** habits. For your own, use past simple:*
- *I wish I **were** more patient.* (not: I wish I would be)

### Wish ≠ future (use "I hope" instead)
- ✗ *I wish you pass the exam.* (not talking about unreal time — wrong)
- ✓ *I **hope** you pass the exam.*

### It's (high) time + subject + past verb
Means something should already have been done — sense of urgency:
- *It**'s high time** we **went** to bed.*
- *It**'s time** we **took** responsibility for our planet.*

### As if / as though + unreal tense
When as if/as though is followed by an **unreal (past) tense**, it shows the speaker doesn't think the statement is really true:
- *Some people behave **as if** their actions **had** no consequences.* (they do have consequences)
- *It was **as though** she **hadn't heard** me.* (she had)

When followed by a present/real tense, the comparison may actually be true:
- *You look **as if** you**'ve** seen a ghost.*

### Would rather + different subjects (unreal tense)
When the two clauses have **different subjects**, use a past (unreal) tense for the second clause:
- *I**'d rather** you **didn't** eat dinner on the new sofa.* (not: didn't → present, but unreal)
- *They **would rather** we **didn't** wait too long before letting them know.*

## 55. AVOIDING REPETITION IN A TEXT

### It / this / that — referring back

| Word | Use | Example |
|------|-----|---------|
| **it** | Neutral reference to something already mentioned | *We watched the episode last night. **It** was OK.* |
| **this / these** | Emphatic, signals something new or interesting; often signals more is coming | *More funding has been approved. **This** does not, however, solve the problem …* |
| **that / those** | Emphatic; suggests **distance** — distancing from an idea or referring further back | *They're idiots! OK, sorry, maybe **that** was unfair.* |

**That / those meaning "the one(s) of"** — in formal English, used to avoid repeating a noun phrase:
- *We compared our experience to **that of** a group of teenagers.* (that = the experience)
- ***Those who** are unable to attend are invited to view it online.* (those who = the people who)

### Such — "of this/that type" (formal)
Used before `a/an + noun` or `plural/uncountable noun` to refer back to a type:
- *Such **navigation methods** are fairly basic.* (= methods of that type)
- *It is the only **such library** in the city.*
- *The **first such meeting** will be held in April.*

### Do so — avoid repeating a verb (formal)
Means "to behave/act in the way mentioned":
- *Our company raised salaries, but few others in the sector **did so**.*
- *Employees who agreed to work overtime should be prepared to **do so** this month.*

### Synonyms
Using different words with the same meaning keeps writing varied and interesting:
- *Air pollution is a significant problem. Urgent action needs to be taken on this worrying **issue**.*
- *Salmon travel back to the river where they were born. These **aquatic homing missiles** can travel hundreds of miles …*

## 56. ELLIPSIS

Leaving out words when the meaning is obvious. Common in informal speech and writing.

### After linkers (and, but, or)
The repeated subject (and be) can be omitted:
- *We had a late dinner and [we] went to bed.*
- *Clare is working a lot [and she's] getting up really early.*
- ⚠ Does NOT work after: `before, after, because, when, while`

### After "to" (infinitive)
Avoid repeating the verb phrase after `to`:
- *A: Have you tried that new restaurant? B: No, I wanted **to** [try it], but it's on the other side of town.*
- *Some people give up sugar, but I didn't want **to** [give it up].*

### After auxiliaries and modals
Avoid repeating the verb phrase:
- *I hadn't noticed it, but my son **had** [noticed it].*
- *They were getting ice creams because I said they **could** [get ice creams].*

### Omitting subject pronouns (obvious from context)
- *[It] Sounds familiar! / [It] Wouldn't have occurred to me.*

### Omitting subject + auxiliary (informal questions)
- *[Do you] Want some coffee? / [Have you] Got a lot of homework?*
- *[Are] You going to the party? / [Will] You give me a ring?*
- ⚠ Does NOT work with `I` or `it`: ✗ *[Am] I given you an idea?*

## 57. EMPHASIS: CLEFT SENTENCES, INVERSION AND AUXILIARIES

### Cleft sentences beginning with "it"
Move the emphasised element to the front after `it + be`:

*You invited me to the party yesterday.* → various emphases:
- ***It was you** who invited me to the party yesterday.* (emphasises: you, not someone else)
- ***It was yesterday** that you invited me to the party.* (emphasises: time)
- ***It was the party** that you invited me to yesterday.* (emphasises: the event)

### Cleft sentences beginning with "what"
`What + clause + be` — emphasises the part **outside** the what clause:
- ***What I like best** about the cinema is talking about the film afterwards.*
- ***What drives me up the wall** is people talking during the film.*

Can also begin with `where, why, who, how`:
- ***How the kids did this** is still unclear to me.*

The what clause can also come at the end:
- *The game we played was **what I liked the most**.*

### Inversion with negative adverbials (formal/persuasive)
Put the adverbial first, then invert subject and auxiliary. If no auxiliary, add one.

- ***Rarely have** I read such an original story.* (I have rarely read…)
- ***Not only do** they have live reptiles but you can also touch them.* (They not only have…)
- ***Little did** I realise the restaurant was about to close.*

### Emphatic auxiliaries (spoken and written)
Stress the auxiliary verb to contradict or correct:
- *A: Why aren't you coming? B: I **am** coming!*

Use `do/does/did` to add emphasis when there's no auxiliary:
- *A: I know you weren't keen. B: I **did** like some of it.*
- *That **does** make sense, actually.*

**Formal imperative with do:** (British English)
- ***Do** sit down, please. / **Do** be quiet!*

## 58. INVERSION AFTER NEGATIVE ADVERBIALS

Inversion = putting the adverbial first + reversing subject–verb order. Used for emphasis, especially in formal or persuasive English.

### Never, rarely, seldom
Often used with present perfect, past perfect, or modals:
- ***Never had** they seen so many people in the village.*
- ***Seldom has** she taken a day off work.*
- ***Rarely can** a patient fully recover from such an injury.*

### Hardly, barely, scarcely + when; No sooner + than
Refer to one event immediately followed by another (usually past perfect):
- ***Hardly had** we sat down **when** we were told to evacuate.*
- ***Scarcely had** the votes been counted **when** the new president was pushed in front of cameras.*
- ***No sooner had** the game started **than** the captain was taken ill.*
- ***No sooner was** the new park open **than** it started raining.*

### Only + time expression
`only after, only if/when, only then, only later`:
- ***Only when** they refilled my glass **did** I realise it was broken.*
- ***Only later did** they discover they hadn't been told the truth.*

### Phrases with no and not
`under no circumstances, on no account, at no time, in no way, on no condition, not until, not only`:
- ***Under no circumstances should** children travel without an adult.*
- ***In no way did** we agree to this.*

### Little (negative/restrictive meaning)
- ***Little do** people realise how hard it is to be a chef.*
- ***Little did** she know then that she would become the company director.*

## 59. INVERSION IN CONDITIONALS

Conditional sentences can be formed without `if` using inversion — more **formal**.

### First conditional → Should + infinitive
Replace `if` with `should`. Makes the situation seem slightly less likely; used for polite offers/requests:

| If-form | Inverted form |
|---------|--------------|
| *If a customer wishes to cancel,* | ***Should** a customer **wish** to cancel,* |
| *If you need a marker pen,* | ***Should** you **need** a marker pen,* |

### Second conditional → Were + (subject) + to + infinitive
Replace `if` with `were`:

| If-form | Inverted form |
|---------|--------------|
| *How would the environment be affected if everyone reduced meat?* | *How would it be affected **were** everyone **to reduce** meat?* |
| *If the Sun disappeared,* | ***Were** the Sun **to disappear**,* |

**If verb = be:** just replace with `were`, no `to + infinitive`:
- *If California were a country …* → ***Were** California a country …*

### Third conditional → Had (subject) + past participle
Remove `if`, invert with `had`:

| If-form | Inverted form |
|---------|--------------|
| *If we had lost that match,* | ***Had** we lost that match,* |
| *If we'd known about it earlier,* | ***Had** we known about it earlier,* |

### Negative inverted conditionals — not after subject
- ***Should** you **not** consent …* (= If you don't consent …)
- ***Were** I **not** good at maths …* (= If I wasn't good at maths …)
- ***Had** I **not** been busy …* (= If I hadn't been busy …)

## 60. PARTICIPLE CLAUSES

Enable more economical expression. The participle and the main verb must share the same subject.

### Present participle clauses (active meaning)

| Use | Example |
|-----|---------|
| **Result of action** | *The bomb exploded, **destroying the building**.* |
| **Reason for action** | ***Knowing she loved reading**, Richard bought her a book.* |
| **Simultaneous action** | ***Standing in the queue**, I realised I had no money.* |
| **Additional info about subject** | ***Starting in the new year**, the policy bans cars in the city centre.* |

### Past participle clauses (passive meaning)

| Use | Example |
|-----|---------|
| **Similar to if condition** | ***Used carefully**, these boots will last for years. (= If used …)* |
| **Reason for action** | ***Worried by the news**, she called the hospital.* |
| **Additional info** | ***Filled with pride**, he walked towards the stage.* |

### Perfect participle clauses (completed before main action)
`having + past participle` — active or passive:
- ***Having got dressed**, he slowly went downstairs.*
- ***Having finished their training**, they will be fully qualified doctors.*
- ***Having been made redundant**, she started looking for a new job.*

### After conjunctions and prepositions
Especially with `-ing`, participle clauses follow: `before, after, instead of, on, since, when, while, in spite of`:
- ***Before cooking**, you should wash your hands.*
- ***Instead of complaining**, they should try doing something positive.*
- ***On arriving** at the hotel, he went to get changed.*
- ***In spite of having read** the instructions twice, I still couldn't understand.*

## 61. POSSESSION AND NOUN MODIFIERS

### Apostrophe 's — when to use with THINGS

Normally we use `of` (not `'s`) with things:
- ✓ *the door of the house* (NOT: the house's door)
- ✓ *the door handle* (noun modifier — NOT: the door's handle)

**Exceptions — 's IS used with things:**

| Context | Example |
|---------|---------|
| Places / groups of people | *our school's cafeteria / the government's policies / different countries' lifestyles* |
| Time expressions | *today's busy world / last week's meeting / a day's journey* |
| The word "life" | *life's stresses / life's little pleasures* |

### Of — for things, ideas, positions
- *in different parts **of** the world*
- *the lifestyles **of** other cultures*
- *the middle **of** winter / the front **of** the house*

### Noun modifiers — first noun acts as adjective
Do NOT use possessive form for noun modifiers:
- ✓ *a film night* (NOT: a film's night)
- ✓ *the winter months* (NOT: the winter's months)
- ✓ *a city bus / an earring*

**What noun modifiers show:**
- Material: *a stone bridge, silver earrings*
- Part of something: *the car door, the chair leg*
- Multiple nouns: *London interior designers / a home office chair*

**Measurements and values as noun modifiers** (units always **singular**):
- *a **ten-minute** break* (not: ten-minutes)
- *a **four-hundred-year-old** tree*
- *a **ten-pound** note*
- *a **fifty-kilometre** journey*
